1 · What it is, who it's for

A real-time training weather display for Fort Rucker Instructor Pilots.

My Weather Pal is a single-pane briefing-room display that consolidates every piece of weather information a Fort Rucker Instructor Pilot needs into one glanceable view. It replaces the workflow of opening four browser tabs (army.mil PDFs, aviationweather.gov METARs, FAA WAAS map, NOAA TAF text) with a single URL that auto-refreshes — main weather data every five minutes, lightning every minute.

Who uses it

  • Pilots — quick GO/NO-GO check before stepping to the aircraft
  • Instructors — visual aid during pre-flight briefings; everyone in the room reads the same data
  • Standardization & Operations — at-a-glance situational awareness during the duty day
  • Schedulers / SDOs — early visibility into deteriorating conditions that may affect periods later in the day

What it answers, instantly

The four questions it's optimized for
  1. Is Cairns AAF GO or NO-GO for VFR training right now?
  2. Are the four stagefields (Allen, Toth, Highbluff, Lucas) trainable, and if not, when will they be?
  3. Can students fly solo today (1200/3, ≤15 kt wind, ≤10 kt gust spread)?
  4. If we have to launch IFR, do we meet taxi mins, do we have legal alternates, and what does the TAF say for the next 12 hours?

Why it exists

The official Cairns Weather products (Mission Execution Forecast and DD Form 175-1) are issued as PDFs three times a day. They contain everything a pilot needs but require reading dense regulatory format and cross-referencing live METAR/TAF data from separate sources. My Weather Pal does that cross-reference automatically and presents the result as a HUD-style training-focused dashboard.

The tool encodes USAACE Regulation 115-1 (4 March 2024) — every threshold, zone definition, and amendment criterion comes from the regulation. It is a pilot reference, not a substitute for official forecaster briefings.

How the pipeline works

End-to-end flow
An always-on server located offsite runs a weather-pal-publisher CLI (Swift) every 5 minutes. The CLI scrapes army.mil for new PDFs, parses them using PDFKit + custom regex (with Vision OCR fallback), fetches live METARs/TAFs/WAAS, and pushes a single data.json file to a public GitHub repository. The dashboard, hosted on GitHub Pages, fetches that JSON every 5 minutes and re-renders. All third-party fetching happens server-side, so the browser only ever talks to GitHub.

Trust and verification

The dashboard always tells you how fresh each piece of data is. You'll see:

  • Published X min ago — when the main publishing server last pushed data.json (runs every 5 min). Colored heartbeat dot: green <10 min, yellow 10–20 min, red >20 min
  • ⚡ Detector — separate health pip for the standalone lightning service (runs every 1 min, independent). green <2 min, yellow 2–5 min, red >5 min. Hidden until the first successful lightning.json fetch.
  • OLD WEATHER banner — appears if the displayed forecast is from a previous calendar day or its valid period has expired
  • USING FORECAST · METAR IS STALE badge — if the live METAR is >80 min old, the dashboard synthesizes conditions from the GOLDFISH area forecast and flags the substitution prominently

The design principle: stale data is always labeled, never silently shown as fresh.

7 · Lightning Detection

Real-time satellite-based lightning awareness, with Cairns's 5 NM ops-halt rule built in.

The dashboard fetches lightning strike data from the GOES-19 Geostationary Lightning Mapper (GLM) — a satellite instrument that watches the entire western hemisphere for lightning flashes 24/7 at 500 frames per second. Every flash within 50 NM of KOZR in the last 10 minutes is summarized and rendered as a top-of-page banner with three severity tiers.

The GOES-19 satellite

GOES-19 (Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellite — 19) is one of NOAA's flagship weather satellites. It launched in June 2024 and took over the GOES-East operational position from GOES-16 in early 2025. From that position it provides continuous observation of the entire Western Hemisphere — the Americas plus a wide ocean buffer to the east and west.

Key facts about its viewpoint:

  • Geostationary orbit: 22,236 miles (35,786 km) above the equator at 75.2°W longitude. At that altitude its orbital period exactly matches Earth's rotation, so it stays fixed over the same patch of ground 24 hours a day.
  • Field of view: the entire visible Earth disk from that vantage — every state, every ocean approach, every storm cell anywhere from Alaska to South America.
  • Continuous operation: never sets, never reorients, never has to be retasked. Lightning detection is its routine job, not something it has to be pointed at.

GOES-19 carries six instruments — visible/infrared imagers, magnetometers, solar imagers, particle detectors, and the lightning mapper. We only use the GLM for this feature; the other channels feed the radar and weather imagery you see on TV news.

How the GLM detects lightning

The Geostationary Lightning Mapper is essentially a very fast wide-field optical camera tuned to one specific wavelength of light: 777.4 nanometers, which is an emission line of atomic oxygen that lightning produces strongly. Other visible light (sun glint, city lights, aircraft strobes) is filtered out by the narrowband filter, so the camera sees lightning and basically nothing else.

GLM specificationValue
Frame rate500 frames per second across the entire hemisphere
Pixel resolution~8 km at the equator (a bit coarser at high latitudes)
Wavelength777.4 nm (atomic oxygen emission line of lightning)
Field of viewFull Earth disk from geostationary orbit
Detection methodPixel-brightness change between consecutive frames (2 ms apart)
Detection efficiency~70–90% of all flashes (cloud-to-ground, intra-cloud, cloud-to-cloud)

This is fundamentally different from ground-based lightning detection networks (such as Vaisala's commercial NLDN), which use radio-frequency triangulation between multiple ground antennas. GLM is purely optical and observes everything from above. The two methods complement each other; the dashboard uses GLM because it's free, public, and covers the entire training area uniformly.

The data hierarchy NOAA publishes

GLM doesn't ship raw camera frames — that would be hundreds of gigabytes per hour. NOAA's processing pipeline groups detected events into a meaningful hierarchy:

LevelDefinitionRoughly equivalent to
Event One pixel firing in one frame (2 ms long) The smallest unit of detection — a brief flash in one location
Group Adjacent events in the same frame, within 16.5 km of each other One branch or arc of a lightning flash
Flash Groups within 16.5 km and 330 ms of each other A single discrete lightning flash (the unit a pilot would call "a strike")

Our processing operates at the flash level — that's what a human observer would count as one bolt of lightning. Every flash record contains its latitude, longitude, start time (microsecond precision), duration, and total optical energy.

NOAA bundles these flash records into NetCDF-4 files (a self-describing scientific data format used widely in atmospheric science) and publishes them with a product name of GLM-L2-LCFA ("Level-2 Lightning Cluster Filter Algorithm"). A new file is written about every 20 seconds, covering the events from that 20-second window. Each file is small (50–500 KB) and contains every flash anywhere in the Western Hemisphere during that window.

How we get it to the dashboard

NOAA partners with Amazon Web Services to host all GOES-19 data in a public S3 bucket — noaa-goes19 — as part of the AWS Open Data registry. That means anyone can read the data with no authentication, no API key, no rate limit, and no cost. We use the public HTTPS interface; the bucket is structured by date and hour:

Bucket layout
https://noaa-goes19.s3.amazonaws.com/GLM-L2-LCFA/{year}/{day-of-year}/{hour}/

Each hour folder contains roughly 180 NetCDF files (one every 20 seconds). Files publish about 10–30 seconds after the events they contain — that's the satellite-to-bucket latency of NOAA's processing pipeline.

What runs on the publishing server

An always-on server located offsite runs a small dedicated process for lightning, separate from the main 5-min weather publisher. The lightning service does this every minute:

  1. List recent files. Query the S3 bucket for both the current and previous hour folders. (We span two hours so we don't miss files when the clock crosses an hour boundary.)
  2. Filter to a 10-minute window. Take only files whose modification timestamp is within the last 10 minutes — typically about 30 files (10 minutes × 3 files per minute).
  3. Download each file. Fetch the NetCDF-4 binary directly into memory (no temporary disk files).
  4. Parse the NetCDF. Extract three arrays from each file: flash_lat, flash_lon, and flash_time_offset. Decode the time offsets against the file's reference epoch (typically "seconds since 2000-01-01 12:00:00 UTC") to get a real timestamp for each flash.
  5. Distance check. For every flash, compute the great-circle distance from KOZR (31.2767°N, 85.7105°W) using the haversine formula. Convert to nautical miles.
  6. Filter to 50 NM. Discard any flash farther than 50 nautical miles from Cairns. This is the outer ring of the dashboard's banner — anything beyond is noise from distant storms.
  7. Bucketize. Count surviving flashes into four concentric bands: ≤5 NM (operations halt), ≤10 NM (vicinity), ≤25 NM (approach), ≤50 NM (outer awareness).
  8. Identify the most recent strike. Sort the surviving flashes by time, take the latest one, and compute its bearing from KOZR (initial great-circle bearing, in degrees from true north).
  9. Build the summary. Assemble a small JSON document: counts in each band, last-strike location/time/distance/bearing, and metadata (lookback window, file count, fetch timestamp).
  10. Push to GitHub. If the new payload is byte-identical to the current lightning.json on the dashboard repo (clear weather, identical zeros), skip the commit. Otherwise, push the new file via the GitHub Contents API. This keeps the repo's commit log clean during quiet periods.

The service uses Python (the de-facto standard for NetCDF processing thanks to the netCDF4 library), runs as a launchd-managed background agent on the publishing server, and logs every run to a dedicated log file. A typical healthy run takes 5–10 seconds end to end (most of which is downloading the ~15 MB of NetCDF data over the public internet).

What runs on the dashboard side

The dashboard polls the public lightning.json file every minute on its own independent schedule (no coordination with the main 5-min cycle). When new data arrives, the dashboard:

  1. Decodes the JSON.
  2. Determines the appropriate banner tier from the count fields: within_5nm > 0 → CRITICAL, otherwise within_10nm > 0 → VICINITY, otherwise within_50nm > 0 → DISTANT, otherwise hide.
  3. Renders the banner with the matching color, status text, and pulse animation.
  4. Populates the count chips and the last-strike line (distance, bearing, compass cardinal).
  5. Starts a once-per-second timer that updates the "X seconds ago" countdown live, so the operator can read freshness without waiting for the next data fetch.

The dashboard does not fetch GLM data directly from AWS — that would require every connected viewer to download ~15 MB of NetCDF every minute, parse it in JavaScript, and individually compute distance/bearing math. By keeping the heavy lifting on the publishing server, all viewers benefit from a single shared fetch/computation, and the dashboard stays responsive on modest hardware (4K TV displays, phones on cellular).

For storm cells moving at the typical 25 knots, even worst-case latency means the storm has only moved about 1.25 NM closer between detection and display — well inside the 5 NM operational ring. The system is fast enough to be operationally trustworthy.

5-mile rule
Cairns's operational policy may halt flight operations when lightning is detected within 5 NM of the field. The CRITICAL tier exists specifically to surface that condition the moment GOES-19 detects a strike inside that radius — but the actual halt call belongs to the duty forecaster reading the KOZR ASOS automated lightning detector, which is the authoritative source. When this banner is red, check the official weather and verify with the forecaster; the dashboard's job is awareness, not enforcement.

What each banner shows

Every tier displays:

  • Last strike location — distance in NM, bearing in degrees, and a cardinal compass direction (e.g. 23.2 NM @ 287° (WNW))
  • Live "X seconds/minutes ago" countdown — ticks every second so you can read freshness at a glance during fast-evolving cells
  • Strike count chips — flash totals at each radius over the last 10-minute window (e.g. 4 ≤5NM · 11 ≤10NM · 38 ≤25NM · 72 ≤50NM)

Update cadence

Lightning runs as an independent service, separate from the main 5-min publisher:

  • Backend fetch: every 60 seconds, the publishing server pulls the most recent GLM data from NOAA's AWS Open Data bucket and pushes a fresh lightning.json to the dashboard repo.
  • Dashboard refresh: the dashboard fetches lightning.json on its own 1-minute interval, separate from the 5-min main data refresh.
  • Live countdown: the "X ago" label updates every second between data fetches.

End-to-end latency: from strike to banner is typically under 2 minutes. GLM has ~30 seconds of detection-to-availability latency on the satellite side; the dashboard adds another minute or so for the polling cycle.

Storm motion tracking

Beyond just counting strikes, the lightning service tracks coherent storm cells over a 30-minute window and projects an ETA to each ring around KOZR (50 / 25 / 10 / 5 NM and the field itself). When a cell is approaching, a third line appears under the banner detail:

🚨 Cell approaching at 28 kt — ~14 min to 5 NM ring MODERATE

The line goes red with a 🚨 icon when the projected ETA to the 5 NM ring is ≤15 minutes — even when the active banner tier is still DISTANT or VICINITY. That way an inbound cell stands out before it crosses into the closer rings.

How it works

Five-step pipeline, all running on the publishing server every minute:

  1. Cluster — every flash in the last 30 minutes within 100 NM of KOZR is grouped into "cells" by spatial proximity (any two flashes within 5 NM of each other belong to the same cell). Implemented via a stdlib Union-Find — no external dependencies.
  2. Time-bin — each cell's flashes are sorted into 5-minute time buckets (0–5 min ago, 5–10, 10–15, …, up to 25–30).
  3. Centroid trajectory — for each bucket with at least 2 flashes, compute the average position. The sequence of centroids over time is the cell's track.
  4. Linear motion fit — least-squares regression on the (time, x) and (time, y) centroid coordinates yields a velocity vector (speed in knots, bearing in degrees from true north).
  5. Project + intersect — closed-form quadratic solve for when the projected path enters each ring radius. The smallest positive root is the first crossing time.

Confidence rating

LabelRequires
HIGH≥30 flashes, ≥5 time buckets, R² ≥ 0.8
MODERATE≥10 flashes, ≥3 buckets, R² ≥ 0.5
LOWBelow MODERATE thresholds — typically not shown

R² is the goodness-of-fit of the linear motion model. A perfectly straight track gives R² = 1.0; a noisy or curving track gives lower values. Cells with R² < 0.5 are dropped entirely — better to say nothing than to commit to an unreliable ETA.

What gets dropped (no prediction shown)

  • Sparse cells — fewer than 10 flashes can't be tracked reliably
  • Brief cells — fewer than 3 time buckets means we don't have enough trajectory points
  • Stationary cells — speed under 5 knots is treated as noise
  • Receding cells — when the motion vector points away from KOZR, no warning is needed
  • Beyond 30 min — even reliable trajectories aren't projected past 30 minutes ahead, because atmospheric dynamics make longer projections unreliable

Operational caveat

Awareness, not prediction-as-fact
Real storm cells split, merge, intensify, and dissipate in ways pure linear motion can't capture. The system is conservative on purpose — when in doubt, it stays silent. A wrong ETA is worse than no ETA. Treat the projection as a heads-up that lets you start preparing, not as a guarantee of when conditions will deteriorate.

When the banner hides

The banner is only shown when there's been at least one flash within 50 NM of KOZR in the last 10 minutes. On clear-weather days, the banner is completely hidden — no chrome, no clutter. It appears the moment a storm develops anywhere in the operating area.

What this is not

  • Not a substitute for the duty forecaster's call. The official 5-NM ops-halt determination is made by the weather forecaster reading the KOZR ASOS automated lightning detector. The dashboard's banner is awareness; their call is authoritative.
  • Not 100% detection. GLM is optical (looks for the 777.4 nm oxygen emission line of lightning). Detection efficiency is high (~70–90%) but flashes deeply embedded in cloud where the optical signal is fully absorbed may be missed.
  • Not a strike map. The banner shows the most-recent strike's distance and bearing, plus a motion-derived ETA when available — but doesn't plot every individual strike. For a full map view, use the Windy.com embed at the bottom of the dashboard.
  • Not a guaranteed ETA. The storm-motion projection assumes linear extrapolation of recent cell motion. Real storms accelerate, decelerate, change direction, and split. The projection is a heads-up, not a hard schedule — verify with the duty forecaster before committing operationally.

8 · Special features

The non-obvious tools tucked into the dashboard.

7-Day Outlook (VFR banner center)

Compact dropdown with 7 day-letters, each carrying a single colored dot summarizing the worst category across that day's AM/PM/N1 periods. Click to expand a detailed 14-period × 7-category grid plus the forecaster's synopsis. Driven by the 06-12-mar-26.pdf-style outlook chart issued daily by Cairns Weather.

KOZR TAF strip (VFR banner)

Six colored hourly cells for the next 6 hours of KOZR TAF, expandable to a 12-hour view. Each cell's color is the FAA flight category for that hour. Falls back to a "RAW TAF" text view when aviationweather.gov has no TAF cached and the parser doesn't have enough structure to color individual hours.

Worst-case per-hour color

The cell color is the worst of the active main group (BECMG / FM / initial) AND any active TEMPO / PROB overlay at that hour. This matches aviationweather.gov's display convention — so a TEMPO TSRA with 2 SM visibility shows the hour as IFR even when the main BECMG has 6+ SM and only mid-level clouds. Operationally this surfaces hours where conditions COULD drop to IFR/LIFR, not just the predominant forecast.

In the expanded 12-hour view, hours where a TEMPO/PROB overlay is driving the worse color get an asterisk on the category label (e.g. IFR*) with a hover-tooltip. Severity ranking: VFR < MVFR < IFR < LIFR.

WAAS / GPS Coverage (IFR banner center)

Real-time FAA WAAS coverage classification for KOZR. The publisher fetches the official FAA snapshot, samples a 17×17 pixel region around Cairns, drops near-white state-border pixels, averages the rest, and threshold-matches against the FAA color ramp. Result is one of:

  • Full LPV200 — best precision approach available (special blue dot + glow)
  • LPV — full precision approach
  • LNAV/VNAV only
  • Marginal
  • Degraded
  • Unknown — sampling failed

9 · Status & failure indicators

How to recognize when something's wrong.

The dashboard never silently shows stale data. Every failure mode has a visible indicator. Here's how to read them:

Heartbeat pip turns red and pulses

What it means
The publishing server (located offsite) hasn't pushed new data in > 20 minutes. Either the server is offline, the publisher process crashed, GitHub auth expired, or the network is down.

What to do: SSH to the publishing host, check the publisher log, verify the launchd agents are loaded. Until restored, the dashboard will keep showing whatever was last published — visibly stale via the pip + age counter.

OLD WEATHER banner (yellow)

What it means
The currently displayed forecast is operationally stale — either issued on a previous calendar day in the operator's local time, or its valid period has already ended (e.g., the N1 brief for last night still showing because today's brief hasn't been issued yet). Yellow 3px viewport border + diagonal striping over forecast-driven sections.

What to do: Treat it as last-known. The METAR / Cairns / IFR Training / Alternate Planning sections still read live data and aren't dimmed.

USING FORECAST · METAR IS STALE (cyan badge)

What it means
The live KOZR METAR is > 80 minutes old. The dashboard has switched to synthesizing conditions from the GOLDFISH area-forecast segment (because Cairns AAF sits inside the GOLDFISH training area). The pseudo-METAR is prefixed FCST so it's obviously not a real observation.

What to do: Auto-reverts as soon as a fresh METAR arrives. No manual action needed.

UNKNOWN status (yellow ❓)

What it means
Cairns shows UNKNOWN when the live METAR is missing entirely AND no GOLDFISH fallback is available either (rare). Stagefields don't go UNKNOWN — they always evaluate against the area forecast which is part of the brief itself.

Active Advisories card glows amber

What it means
At least one AWA/TWA/WATCH/WARNING is currently in effect. Multiple stack vertically inside the card; each shows expiry countdown ("Expires in 1h 24m"). An "Upcoming" banner above the active list previews the next pending advisory (only the soonest is shown).

Old Cairns Weather data > 24 hours

Day-after-day (e.g., a holiday weekend) the OLD WEATHER banner persists across calendar boundaries. The publisher will keep retrying army.mil every 5 minutes, silently — once a new brief lands the banner clears automatically.
